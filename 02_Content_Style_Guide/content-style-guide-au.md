# Content Style Guide (AU English)
## BYD AU Warranty & Service Documentation
Version 1.0  
Owner: Technical Documentation & Pre-Sales Operations  

---

# 1. Purpose

This style guide defines linguistic, structural, and formatting standards for warranty and service documentation intended for the Australian market.

The objective is to ensure:

- Clarity for non-technical customers
- Consistency across vehicle models
- Regulatory-aligned tone
- Terminology control
- DITA-compatible structured authoring

This guide applies to:

- Warranty handbooks
- Service interval documentation
- Pre-sales technical materials
- Compliance-facing content
- Customer support knowledge base topics

---

# 2. Language Standard

## 2.1 Regional Standard

All English documentation must follow **Australian English (en-AU)** conventions.

### Examples

| Correct (AU) | Avoid |
|--------------|--------|
| authorised | authorized |
| centre | center |
| programme | program (when referring to structured initiatives) |

Note: “Program” may be used in technical or software contexts where industry standard requires it.

---

## 2.2 Tone and Voice

Documentation must be:

- Formal
- Neutral
- Compliance-aware
- Non-promotional

Avoid:

- Marketing exaggeration
- Absolute guarantees
- Informal language

Correct:
> Warranty coverage is subject to applicable terms and conditions.

Avoid:
> We guarantee full protection in all situations.

---

# 3. Sentence Structure Rules

## 3.1 Sentence Length

- Recommended maximum: 25–30 words per sentence.
- Avoid multiple nested clauses.
- Prefer active voice when clarity improves understanding.

Example:

✔ Correct:
> Customers must complete scheduled servicing within the required interval.

✖ Avoid:
> Customers are expected to ensure that servicing, which may vary depending on usage patterns, is completed in accordance with applicable documentation.

---

## 3.2 Clarity for Non-Technical Audience

- Avoid unexplained acronyms.
- Define technical terms on first occurrence.
- Prefer plain language when possible.

Example:
> Battery Management System (BMS)

---

# 4. Terminology Control

## 4.1 Controlled Vocabulary

All terms must align with the approved TBX termbase.

- Do not introduce synonyms without approval.
- Do not translate technical components inconsistently.
- Use defined abbreviations only after first expansion.

---

## 4.2 Legal & Risk Language

Avoid:

- "Guaranteed"
- "Always"
- "Never"

Use:

- "Subject to review"
- "May affect"
- "In accordance with"

---

# 5. Numeric & Measurement Standards

## 5.1 Units

- kWh (not KWh)
- km (not KM)
- °C (not C degrees)

Format:

- Space between number and unit: 60 kWh
- No space before %: 80%
- Thousands separator: 10,000 km

---

## 5.2 Date Format

Preferred format:

- 15 February 2025
- DD Month YYYY

Avoid:

- 02/15/2025 (US format)

---

# 6. DITA Structural Conventions

## 6.1 Topic Types

- Concept: explains what something is
- Task: explains how to perform an action
- Reference: factual data only

Each topic must:

- Contain one primary purpose
- Avoid mixing procedural and descriptive content
- Be reusable across vehicle models

---

## 6.2 Modular Writing Principles

- One idea per paragraph
- No cross-topic duplication
- Avoid model-specific language unless necessary
- Separate compliance disclaimers into reusable components

---

# 7. Formatting Standards

## 7.1 Headings

- Sentence case for headings
- Avoid ALL CAPS
- No trailing punctuation

## 7.2 Lists

- Use bullet lists for unordered items
- Use numbered lists for sequential steps
- Keep list items grammatically parallel

---

# 8. Translation Considerations (EN ↔ ZH)

- Maintain neutral tone in both languages.
- Avoid overinterpretation of conditional statements.
- Preserve modal verbs (may / must / should) accurately.

Example:

English:
> Warranty coverage may be affected.

Chinese:
> 质保资格可能受到影响。

Not:
> 质保资格不一定有效。

---

# 9. Version Control

Every document must include:

- Version number
- Last updated date
- Owner department
- Change summary

---

# 10. Compliance Objective

This style guide ensures that documentation is:

- Legally safe
- Terminology-consistent
- Structurally modular
- Readable by non-technical customers
- Ready for audit and regulatory review
